Before we begin: I'm not against love. The following is a structural analysis, not a breakup letter. If you're currently in a relationship, I hope it's going well. This piece is probably more useful when it's not.

The real world runs on fat tails. In careers, companies, scientific breakthroughs — a tiny number of extreme outcomes account for most of the value. The correct strategy in that world is specific: run many experiments in parallel, keep each one reversible and cheap, stay alive long enough to catch a tail. Not a preference. The only approach that works.

Love looks like a fat tail opportunity. The exceptional relationship isn't marginally better than a mediocre one — it's categorically different. The right person changes what you're capable of: what you build, how you think, who you're becoming. That compounds over decades in ways no component analysis captures. The upside is real.

So is the left tail. The worst relationships aren't disappointing. They're years of psychological damage, identity erosion, trauma that takes longer to surface than it took to accumulate. The distribution has weight at both ends. The middle — the merely fine — is emptier than you'd expect.

The problem isn't that love is fat-tailed. The problem is that love's structure makes it impossible to run the strategy.

## Gold standard. Still fails.

Let's say you try to solve this with better selection. The most rigorous selection process available: CEO hiring. Professional search firms. Structured interviews refined over decades. Background checks. Board-level collective decisions. Partially quantifiable track records.

Median S&P 500 CEO tenure: five to seven years. Already priced in — nobody expects a CEO to last forever. You're trying to hire for life.

But there's a harder problem than selection. People change. The person you chose correctly at year zero is a different person at year ten. A CEO who has changed can be walked through a defined exit process. What's the equivalent for a partner?

This is why selection failure and the change problem don't add together — they multiply. Your process for identifying the right person is already worse by several orders of magnitude than the best corporate hiring apparatus. And the thing you're selecting is itself a moving target, with no efficient mechanism to adjust if it drifts. Every error in selection gets compounded by every year of change you can't anticipate or exit cleanly.

## The iteration trap.

In a fat tail world, the optimal strategy has a name: run many experiments in parallel, keep each one cheap and reversible, get signal fast, iterate. Not a product philosophy — a survival strategy. The only way to catch a tail when you don't know which experiment will hit is to run as many as possible, as cheaply as possible, with exits that actually work.

Now apply this to love.

The minimum viable test for whether two people work is months, and that's before any real stress. You cannot run relationships in parallel with genuine investment in each — the architecture doesn't support it. And when something isn't working, you cannot break up at 9am and be done by 10. Even if you want out, you can't get out immediately. That's not a metaphor. It's just true.

Minimum cycle time: irreducibly long. Parallelism: off the table. Clean exits: don't exist. You're not just failing to iterate faster — you're structurally blocked from running the only strategy that works in the world you're living in. Both tails are in play on every attempt, and you have one shot at a time to find out which you drew.

## The last monopoly.

If love is this structurally bad, why has it been the default for so long?

Because it was a monopoly.

Monopolies don't need to be good value. When there's no substitute, you pay whatever's charged. Community and religion used to share the emotional presence market — but urbanization, secularization, and the steady atomization of modern life had already eroded those. Love was the last holdout. So what was it actually holding?

Strip it down. A partner provides three things: money, emotional support, sex. Money: getting someone significantly richer than you to spend willingly on you is a skill, and a niche one — mostly not the point. Sex: product development in this category has been, let's say, thorough. Silicon isn't losing to carbon anymore. Emotional support breaks into two layers: daily care, which the service economy mostly handles, and psychological presence — consistent, unconditional, available at 3am on a weeknight. That last one was the protected market. No therapist is on call at midnight for free. No friend owes you that. That was love's moat.

AI is the first thing that actually crosses it. Not because it replicates love's depth — but because it is the first substitute that works on the right terms: low cost, no exclusivity, no minimum commitment, available in parallel with everything else in your life. Previous substitutes existed. What didn't exist was a substitute you could access without replicating love's structural costs. That's the mechanism. That's why this is different.

The trend started before the technology: falling marriage rates, rising single-person households, "I'm actually fine on my own" migrating from eccentric to unremarkable. AI is the last piece — it opens the one protected market that was still holding.

Love doesn't disappear. It reclassifies: from mass-market default to niche luxury.

Like wingsuit flying. Terrible risk-adjusted returns. High fatality rate. Enormous time cost before you're even competent. People who do it want to do it again immediately. There's no shared vocabulary between someone who jumps and someone who doesn't. And you can't run a test flight without the full stakes. That's not a design flaw. It's the whole design.

I know some people win. The casino has winners. Some of them win millions. I'm not arguing it's impossible. What I'm arguing is that the structure is against you — and unlike most investments, the cost of finding out whether you're one of the winners is indistinguishable from the cost of being wrong. You can only know if you found the right person by already being in deep. The search cost and the commitment cost are the same cost.

So I don't go to the casino. Most people, increasingly, don't either.
