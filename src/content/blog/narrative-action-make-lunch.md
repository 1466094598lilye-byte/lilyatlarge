The minimum viable unit of changing your life is making something you're going to eat. Not a vision board. Not a morning routine. Not a conversation with a therapist, though that can also help. A bowl of instant noodles, if that's what you have. PB&J. Toast. The bar is genuinely that low, and I'm going to spend the next few thousand words explaining why this is actually the conclusion of a rigorous argument and not a sign that I've run out of ideas.

The argument starts with a physicist who built a map of all possible universes, runs through a Chinese writer who read him carefully, involves three failed attempts at a philosophical debate, and ends in your kitchen. Bear with me.

---

## I. Why This Universe Lets Us Tell Stories

Our universe is not the only possible one.

Physicist Stephen Wolfram proposed a concept called the Ruliad — the entangled limit of all possible computations, every conceivable set of physical laws, every universe that could exist. Ours is one slice of that totality. The question is: why this slice?

The easy answer is the anthropic principle: this universe seems special because we're in it — which is roughly the cosmological equivalent of explaining why you're remarkable by pointing out that you exist. But the writer 万维钢, reading Wolfram, found something more interesting. Our universe is this universe because it satisfies one specific requirement: it can support narrative.

Narrative — a coherent account of causally connected events — turns out to be demanding to support. Think through what even a simple story needs.

The first requirement: causality has to be real and consistent across observers. If A causes B, every observer has to agree that A came first — not most observers, not observers in convenient positions, but all of them. If causal order varies by perspective, two people can't reason together in the same universe; they're not even agreeing on which direction time runs. How does a universe guarantee this? By capping how fast information travels. If signals could propagate instantaneously, you could construct scenarios where A precedes B in one reference frame and B precedes A in another — causality becomes a matter of opinion, which is fine for Twitter but fatal for physics. Special relativity's speed-of-light limit isn't primarily a claim about speed; it's a structural guarantee for cause and effect. Narrative requires consistent causality; consistent causality requires a maximum information speed; that constraint generates the architecture of special relativity.

The second requirement: the future has to be genuinely open, and the past genuinely closed. A universe where everything is in principle determined from initial conditions has no room for narrative — no agency, no choice, no "and then I decided to." Classical physics gave us exactly that universe: Laplace's demon, knowing all positions and velocities, could compute everything forward and backward. The demon knew what you'd have for breakfast before you were born. This is either a profound insight or a deeply inconvenient one, depending on how attached you are to the concept of deciding things. Quantum mechanics broke it. A particle's state before measurement isn't just unknown — it has no definite value. The uncertainty isn't a gap in our instruments; it's a feature of reality. And once something happens, it's fixed: the past is solid, the future is real. A universe where what's done is done and what comes next is genuinely undetermined — which is the only kind of universe where a story can happen.

The third requirement: there has to be something to narrate. A universe of particles dispersing uniformly into void has no characters, no settings, nothing for a story to be about. Gravity interrupts that drift — pulling matter together into structures that persist. Stars, planets, a table, a person. Without it, everything disperses, nothing persists, and the universe is maximally boring in a very thorough way.

So: narrative requires consistent causality → special relativity. Narrative requires an open future and a fixed past → quantum mechanics. Narrative requires persistent structure → gravity and general relativity.

Turn it around: physical laws don't happen to permit narrative. Narrative's requirements specify what the physical laws must look like. Narrative is the prior.

I accept this. But the conclusion 万维钢 draws from it is too tame — which is where the argument gets interesting.

## II. Can Narratives Be Better or Worse?

Narrative is the interface between us and reality — fine. 万维钢's prescription, once he's established this, is: since all narrative is subjective, you can step outside the narratives others have given you and consciously choose your own. He calls this the fundamental act of modern awakening, which is either genuinely liberating or the kind of thing you say right before you don't answer the harder question.

Because here's the harder question: why is the narrative *you* choose better than the one you inherited?

If all narratives are subjective choices, then "I'm going to consciously choose my narrative" is itself a subjective choice — absorbed from some culture, some era, some set of values you didn't invent. You think you've stepped outside; you've moved from one narrative into another. The self doing the stepping is itself a narrative construction. You haven't escaped; you've redecorated and called it enlightenment.

This matters, because if you take narrative-as-first-principle seriously, you have no position outside all narratives. Which makes the question real: can narratives actually be better or worse than each other? Can one be evaluated against another?

The natural answer: good narratives survive contact with reality. "If I cut prices, customers will come back" makes a prediction; the prediction fails; bad narrative. But in 2011, Netflix split its streaming and DVD businesses and the stock fell 75%. Scoring "streaming is the future" against reality at that moment, you'd declare it dead. It turned out to be right. Reality verification depends entirely on when you're sampling, and the standard offers no guidance on that — which means it's less a standard and more an aesthetic preference for narratives that happen to be winning right now.

Look at structure instead of outcomes, then. A good narrative is falsifiable — it accepts feedback, can be updated. "Everything happens for a reason" absorbs every outcome and predicts nothing. Bad narrative. But consider fortune-telling. Fortune-telling is completely unfalsifiable — "you'll have financial luck next year" — if money comes, the fortune was right; if it doesn't, you failed to seize your opportunity. By any falsifiability standard, fortune-telling is epistemologically worthless and, in practice, occasionally an excellent life coach. People get motivated by fortune-tellers; they take meetings they'd been avoiding, follow through on things they'd been deferring. Its function isn't prediction — it's activation, and at activation it sometimes genuinely works. Grading it on falsifiability is like failing a hammer for not being a screwdriver.

So try a decision standard: what's the expected value of holding this narrative? In 2021, people taking on leverage to buy property at peak prices had done this calculation. "Property hasn't declined in twenty years; the government is incentivized to prevent collapse; land finance guarantees continued appreciation." Internally consistent. Positive expected value — computed entirely inside the narrative that was about to end. That's the problem: expected value isn't external to narrative. It's produced by narrative. You calculate expected value using your narrative, then use that expected value to validate your narrative. The circle has no exit and no sign acknowledging that it's a circle.

Every foothold I reach for turns out to be narrative underneath. There's no outside. The self that would step outside to evaluate is itself something narrative assembled. 万维钢 says this almost in passing, but it's the hardest part of the whole argument — not difficult to escape from narrative, impossible — and the interesting question is: if that's true, what actually goes wrong?

## III. The Problem Isn't Narrative — It's Being Stuck in One

The answer isn't that people are living in someone else's narrative. It's that they're living in only one.

The student who believes academic performance is the only thing that matters — when they fail an exam, it's not a setback, it's a cosmological event. Their entire sense of self lives at one address, and the landlord just changed the locks. Now think about someone with the same academic intensity who also builds things, or coaches, or plays in a band. When the exam goes badly, something absorbs the blow. The rest of them is still standing. They can switch.

The problem isn't the academic narrative. Believing that grades matter is, in most educational contexts, a reasonable position. The problem is that it's the only one — which is the psychic equivalent of a load-bearing wall that's also your living room, your kitchen, and the one thing standing between you and structural collapse. When a single narrative carries the full weight of your identity, any threat to it is a threat to everything. Single-narrative people don't fail. They collapse, and then they have to rebuild the entire house.

This reframes what learning actually does. Reading across domains, picking up frameworks from history and engineering and biology and wherever else — you're not converging on a single correct worldview. You're building redundancy. Engineers call it fault tolerance. The point is that when one story breaks, you're not standing in the rubble of everything you had. You have other rooms.

More distinct narratives in the repertoire means any single one can fail without bringing down the whole structure — diversification, except the assets are stories and what you're protecting isn't money, it's the capacity to keep functioning when one of them stops being true. Which leads to the next question: what makes some narratives so much more durable than others?

## IV. How a Narrative Gets Heavy

Narrative inventory explains resilience. It doesn't explain drive.

Musk has survived more public failures than most people have had difficult weeks. The obvious interpretation — that he's all-in, single-threaded, narrative inventory of exactly one — can't be right. We just established that single-narrative people are the most fragile. So what's different?

The anchor.

The student's narrative — "I am a good student" — is anchored to a result. Any single exam can invalidate it. Musk's narrative — "I am the person making humanity multiplanetary" — is anchored to a direction. SpaceX's first three rockets exploded. Tesla nearly went bankrupt twice. None of this falsified the narrative, because the narrative isn't a claim about outcomes. It's a claim about direction. Failure becomes a chapter, not a verdict. This is either a profound structural insight or a very convenient thing to believe when your rocket is on fire — and the answer is: both.

But there's something beyond anchor structure. When SpaceX's third rocket failed, Musk famously wept in a parking lot — which is not an activity that historically precedes the founding of a successful aerospace company, and yet — then funded a fourth launch. That fourth rocket existed in physical reality. The company, the engineers, the infrastructure, the burned metal of the three failures: all of it was in the world. His narrative had become matter.

Call it narrative intensity: the degree to which a narrative has been instantiated in physical reality. A narrative that lives only in your head is fragile — one good push and it's gone. But a narrative that has been translated into action, into objects and structures and institutions, has a different kind of weight. You can question Musk's goals. You cannot argue with the rocket on the pad. It's there. It doesn't care what you think.

This is also why "just think positively" is the advice equivalent of telling someone their house is on fire and suggesting they visualize it not being on fire. A narrative with no physical output gets more fragile over time, not less — it needs constant external validation because it has nothing of its own to stand on. Each action that translates narrative into physical reality, however small, gives it a foothold outside your own skull. Harder to dislodge. More real.

Which brings the problem that's been underneath this whole argument.

## V. What Starts the Flywheel

If action gives narrative weight, and narrative gives action direction, one of them has to go first. Which one?

The hopeful answer — start anywhere, build momentum — is partially right and mostly useless as instruction. Some people accumulate small wins and plateau. Others accumulate small wins, medium wins, and then something suspiciously large, in a pattern that suggests the flywheel was always going to keep spinning. The mechanism of action-generates-narrative-generates-action is real. It just doesn't tell you what to do at the beginning, which is precisely when you most need to be told what to do.

The first push doesn't come from narrative. It comes from friction.

There's a difference between diffuse dissatisfaction and specific friction. "Life feels meaningless" is diffuse — it doesn't point anywhere, spreads into everything, and is excellent at paralyzing you while feeling urgent. People can live in diffuse dissatisfaction for years. Many do. Some write essays about it. "Why does this specific thing work this way when it so obviously doesn't have to" — that's specific friction. It has a target. It generates a response the way an itch generates scratching, without requiring you to first construct a philosophical position on the value of scratching.

Musk didn't start with a vision of multiplanetary civilization. He looked at what Russians were charging for a rocket, calculated he could build one for a tenth of the cost, and thought: obviously I should just do that. Not a grand narrative — an engineer encountering a specific irritant. The narrative came later, assembled from the weight of the responses.

What this means in practice: the smallest viable action isn't "set a goal" or "find your purpose" or spend seventeen minutes each morning writing three things you're grateful for while the problem sits outside the door, very patiently, not going anywhere.

It's make something you're going to eat.

## Coda

Not "go watch someone make food" — make it yourself. Instant noodles. PB&J. Toast. Whatever you have. The bar is not metaphorically low; it's literally: boil water, or don't even do that.

Here's why this works, and why it's not a deflection: you start with a real need — hunger, which your body has been generating without your input and will continue to generate regardless of your narrative situation. You take an action — make food — that is almost impossible to fail at in any meaningful sense. You get a real result — you're less hungry — that exists in the physical world and cannot be argued with. The loop closes. The narrative gets a foothold outside your head.

The argument of this entire piece has been that narrative doesn't precede action; it accumulates from action. That the self is not a stable entity who chooses narratives from outside, but a moving thing that stories get built around. That the entry point isn't conscious choice — it's specific friction, physical output, matter in the world.

Making lunch enacts the argument rather than just describing it.

万维钢 is right that narrative is the universe's first principle. He's right that we live inside it, not outside it. But the move he describes — step outside, choose consciously — requires a vantage point that has never existed and will not become available if you try harder. The self that would step outside is itself something narrative assembled, and it has no place to stand.

What it does have: a kitchen. A real need. A kettle that takes two minutes to boil.

The first narrative doesn't start with a decision. It starts with putting the kettle on.
